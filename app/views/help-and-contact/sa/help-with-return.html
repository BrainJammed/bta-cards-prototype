Help with your Self Assessment tax return

Self Assessment tax returns

Self Assessment is a system HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) uses to collect Income Tax.

Income Tax is usually deducted automatically from wages, pensions and savings.

People and businesses with other income must report it by filing a Self Assessment tax return.

Self Assessment is used by:

  • the self-employed
  • sole traders
  • individuals such as company directors
  • partners in partnerships
  • trustees
  • other similar people

If you receive a tax return, or a notice to file, you must complete a return and submit it to HMRC.

Who must file a Self Assessment tax return (opens in new tab)

If you must file a tax return, you do so after the end of the tax year it applies to.

The Self Assessment tax year is 6 April to 5 April. For the tax year 6 April 2020 to 5 April 2021, you must file your return by 31 January 2022.

You need to register for Self Assessment if:

  • you must file a tax return for 2020 to 2021

and

  • you did not file one for the tax year 2019 to 2020

You should allow up to 20 working days if you have never filed an online tax return before.

To file your tax return online, you should:

  • be registered for Self Assessment
  • add Self Assessment to your tax account
  • complete and file your tax return by 31 January 2022
  • pay what you owe by 31 January 2022

If you do not register, file or pay on time, you may have to pay a penalty.

If you have not received a notice to file a Self Assessment tax return, you can check anonymously if you need to file a return (opens in new tab).

Why you must complete and file a Self Assessment tax return

You will usually be sent a tax return or a letter about a tax return if you are registered as self-employed.

You must file your tax return even if you have no tax to pay. If you do not file it by the deadline, you may have to pay a penalty.

If you do not need to complete and file a Self Assessment tax return

If you used to file a tax return but do not need to file one for the most recent tax year, tell HMRC to stop Self Assessment.

You must also tell us as soon as you have stopped being self-employed.

Your first Self Assessment tax return

A tax return is a form on which you:

  • report details of your taxable income
  • claim tax allowances and tax reliefs

HMRC may issue a tax return to you each tax year. The Self Assessment tax year runs from 6 April one year to 5 April the next year.

If you receive a tax return, or a notice to file (SA316), the law says you must complete it.

We use the information on your tax return to work out your tax bill or whether you are due a tax refund.

Viewing your Self Assessment calculation

HMRC’s online tax return automatically works out how much you need to pay. You fill in your figures and once you have checked that everything is correct, you can view your calculation.

The calculation shows:

  • how your tax bill has been worked out
  • your income
  • your personal allowance
  • the tax due
  • any class 2 and class 4 National Insurance contributions (opens in new tab)

You can print a copy of the calculation for your records.

Basic record keeping when you’re self-employed

You must record any money going in and out of your business, whether the transaction is made:

  • in person
  • by phone
  • online

Keep your records on paper or digitally using a computer. These include items such as invoices, sale receipts, proof of purchases and bank statements. Remember your records must be accurate, clear and complete.

Some software suppliers offer free record keeping apps for small businesses. How you keep your records will depend on whether you use the traditional or cash basis method of accounting, and how you work out your expenses.

You’ll find it easier if you organise your income and expenses into categories and update these regularly. Remember to back up your files if you’re keeping your records digitally.

You must keep your business and personal transactions separate. It is not an allowable business expense to use money from your business for personal purposes.

If you’re VAT registered, you’ll also need to keep records of how much VAT you’ve paid your suppliers and charged your customers.

If your income is above the VAT threshold, you’ll need to submit your records to us using Making Tax Digital software.

You must keep your records for at least 5 years after the 31 January submission deadline of the relevant tax year. HMRC may check your records to make sure you’re paying the right amount of tax.

Example: For the tax year 2019 to 2020, the submission deadline is 31 January 2021. Keep your records until at least 31 January 2026.
